Molecular Orbital Data Formats

We describe here the format of the various data types found in the database. Below we detail

  • Molecular orbital data, in NIST_orbitals/ and full_orbitals/
  • Ionization cross sections, in ion_xs/
  • Astrochemical network files, in networks/

Orbital data

The orbital information is stored in two directories containing the orbitals in the NIST format, NIST_orbitals/ and including all orbitals, full_orbitals/. An example of the NIST formatted file, NIST_oribtals/co.norb:

 #CO NIST Orbitals
 #Orbital      B            U      N Q
 1        562.416986   794.326705  2    1
 2        309.246847   436.294142  2    1
 3         41.332626    78.262641  2    1
 4         21.888866    71.857734  2    1
 5         17.015000    53.945104  2    1
 6         17.015000    53.945104  2    1
 7         14.266000    42.788163  2    1

where the B column gives the eBE (eV), U gives the average electron kinetic energy (eV), N gives the orbital occupation number and Q is a scaling factor to include higher-order ionization effects. These files can be directly and more readily used to compute the BEB cross sections.

An example of a full orbital file, which provides more complete information, is full_orbitals/co.forb:

 #CO Full Orbitals
 #Alpha orbitals
 #Orb.         KE       BE-HF   BE-P3+     Pole
    1   794.32671   562.41699      ***      ***
    2   436.29414   309.24685      ***      ***
    3    78.26264    41.33263      ***      ***
    4    71.85773    21.88887      ***      ***
    5    53.94510    17.35920   17.015    0.903
    6    53.94510    17.35920   17.015    0.903
    7    42.78816    15.11922   14.266    0.910

where the file will give the information for both alpha and beta orbitals, for open-shell molecules that were computed with an unrestricted formalism. The KE and BE-HF columns give the average electron kinetic energy and eBE (eV) computed by the population analysis in {Gaussian16} following geometry optimization. The latter 2 columns include the results from EPT, where *** denotes orbitals where EPT was not computed. For orbitals where it was computed, we include the EPT-corrected HF canonical orbital energies (at P3+ level) results and the pole strength (PS). For the NIST orbital file, we include the BE-P3+ in the PS > 0.8, following recommendation from Gaussian.

Cross-section data

Cross sections are found in the ion_xs/ directories for the SCAR, HF, MP2 and CCSD(T) calculations and atoms. Cross-section files in these folders are formatted as in the following example, ion_xs/mp2/CO.xs:

 # CO cross section
 256
 1.00000e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.04618e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.09450e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.14505e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.19793e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.25325e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.31113e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
 1.37169e+01     0.00000e+00     0.00000e+00
                 {more data}
 9.13659e+05     8.52230e-03     6.30487e-03
 9.55855e+05     8.17497e-03     6.04801e-03
 1.00000e+06     7.84171e-03     5.80155e-03

where the first row gives the number of electron energy bins. The later rows give the data in (two) three columns. For BEB cross sections, the columns provide electron impact energy (eV), BEB cross section in units of \(\sigma_0^2\) and, if available, the damped-BEB cross section in units of \(\sigma_0^2\). For SCAR cross sections, the two columns provide energy (eV) and SCAR cross section in units of \(\sigma_0^2\).

We also provide a folder called recommended/ which provides a single file for each molecular in the database. These are formatted as above with two columns, as in ion_xs/recommended/CO.xs:

Astrochemical network files

We provide reaction rate files for all molecules in two common astrochemical network formats:

  • KIDA (for Kinetic Database for Astrochemistry). The network formats are described in the KIDA presentation paper, Wakelam et al. (2012), see also their website.
  • UMIST Database for Astrochemistry (UDfA, or more commonly UMIST, sometimes referenced as RateXX, where XX is the year of the release). See their website for formatting.

Note of caution. When including these ionization processes in astrochemical networks, ensure that the network not only includes the molecular ion, but also the recombination rate. Failure to do this will create species with only sources but no important sinks!

We are currently working with KIDA to get all reaction rate data to be included in their online database.
