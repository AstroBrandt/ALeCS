Computing Ionization Rates

Definition of an ionization rate and ionization rate coefficient

For the purposes of astrochemical cosmic-ray ionization rates, for a slab geometry, we define an ionization rate of some molecule, \(m\), by impacting particle, \(i\), as \[ \zeta_m = 2 \pi \int j_i(E_i) \sigma_{m, {\rm ion}}(E_i) dE_i. \] For three-dimensional geometries, the prefactor in front is usually replaced by \(4\pi\).

The above expression works if you have knowledge of the spectrum. However, in most astrochemical applications, the user instead only provides an ionization rate of either atomic or molecular hydrogen. These can either be just the so-called “primary” ionization rate, which accounts only for primary protons, or the “total” ionization rate which includes both primary protons and secondary electrons, or sometimes also primary electrons. In a particular astrochemical network, the rates are then given as \[ \zeta_m = \alpha_m \zeta_0 \] where \(\zeta_0\) is a given reference rate, which may depend on the database used. Therefore, the user should be careful which ionization rate is used in both the chemistry and heating in their code. In our database, we report the \(\alpha_m\) (denoted \(c_{m, T}\) in the paper) using the total ionization rate including both protons and electrons, with the reference value is the total ionization rate of molecular hydrogen, H2. See review in the chapter Cosmic rays: physics, chemistry, and computational challenges Padovani & Gaches (2023), in the recent (and highly recommended) book Astrochemical Modeling: Practical Aspects of Microphysics in Numerical Simulations.

Irradiation fluxes used in the database

The primary interest of this database is for astrochemical modeling, which generally normalized the ionization rates to reference interstellar mediun (ISM) rates.

For computing the above ionization rates, we use both a primary proton and primary electron interstellar spectrum which reproduces diffuse ISM inferred ionization rates (Padovani et al. 2018). Secondary electrons are computing following the more rigorous and complete theory from Ivlev et al. (2021) including important losses from vibrational H2 excitations (see Padovani et al. 2022). We include the depth-depending ionization rates, and then average over typical depths of molecular clouds.

Depth-dependent ionization rates

The depth-dependent electron ionization rate of a molecule, \(m\), is given by adding the contributions of primary protons, \(\zeta_{p, m}(N_{\rm H_2})\), using the approximation \({m_e}E_p \approx {m_p}E_e\),
\[ \zeta_{p,m}(N_{\rm H_2}) = 2\pi \int j_{p}(E_p, N_{\rm H_2}) \,\sigma_{m}\left[(m_e/m_p) E_p\right]\,\mathrm{d}E_p, \] primary electrons, \[ \zeta_{e,m}(N_{\rm H_2}) = 2\pi \int j_{e}(E_e, N_{\rm H_2}) \,\sigma_{m}(E_e)\,\mathrm{d}E_e, \] and secondary electrons from both primary protons and electrons \[ \zeta_{se,m}(N_{\rm H_2}) = 4\pi \int j_{se}(E_e, N_{\rm H_2}) \,\sigma_{m}(E_e)\,\mathrm{d}E_e, \] where \(se\) represents secondary electrons. The factors of \(2\pi\) and \(4\pi\) account for fluxes which are assumed to be plane parallel and isotropic, respectively. The total ionization rate for a molecule is then, \(\zeta_{m} = \zeta_{p,m} + \zeta_{e,m} + \zeta_{se,m}\). The factor of \(4\pi\) comes from treating the secondary electrons as an isotropic local source.

The column-dependent total ionization rate coefficient for species \(m\), \(c_{m, T}(N_{\rm H_2})\) is \[ \zeta_{m, T}(N_{\rm H_2}) = c_{m, T}(N_{\rm H_2})\,\zeta_{{\rm H_2},T}(N_{\rm H_2}). \]

This is computed for a sample of column densities from \(N({\rm H_2}) = 10^{20} - 10^{23}\) cm-2. The average rate coefficient reported in the database is \[ \alpha_m = \bar{c}_{m, T} = \langle c_{m, T}(N_{\rm H_2}) \rangle. \]
